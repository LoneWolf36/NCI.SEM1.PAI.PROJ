1. Overview
This project is designed to evaluate the learning objectives of the Programming for AI module. Students are expected to design and implement a data-driven AI project that addresses a clearly motivated and novel question, demonstrates technical competence, and communicates meaningful findings with academic rigour.
The project should go beyond simply applying standard tools to a readily available dataset. Strong submissions will show originality in problem framing, creativity in combining or exploiting datasets, thoughtful model design, and a convincing explanation of why the work matters.
Assessment Type

Submission Format

3. Project Objective and Scope
The objective of this project is to identify and carry out a series of analyses on a collection of datasets that are related to, or complementary with, one another by using appropriate programming languages, programming environments, and database systems.
Your project should answer a clear research or application question. That question must be novel in its framing, practically relevant, and supported by an argument explaining why the problem is significant and what impact the proposed work could have.
Minimum scope requirements:
Three or four structured or semi-structured datasets must be used.
Each dataset should contain at least 1,000 records.
Datasets must be programmatically stored in appropriate database(s) prior to processing.
Programmatic pre-processing, transformation, analysis, and visualisation of the data are required.
Processed output data should also be programmatically stored in appropriate databases where relevant.
The full workflow should show a coherent end-to-end process rather than isolated scripts.
For example, you could use Python to programmatically retrieve semi-structured datasets (such as XML or JSON), or web-scraped or streaming data, store them in an appropriate database management system, transform them into structured forms, and then apply analysis or AI techniques to discover meaningful patterns. However, stronger projects will also demonstrate why the chosen problem is interesting, what makes the approach distinctive, and how the output could be useful.

5. Deliverables
5.1 Project Report
The objectives, methodology, and results of your analysis should be presented in the form of a project report. The report should discuss the programming, modelling, and data-processing challenges encountered, together with the decisions you made to address them.
The report should be around 3,000 words in length (excluding references), should use appropriate academic style and referencing, and should be presented in IEEE conference format. Templates for Microsoft Word and LaTeX can be downloaded from:
https://www.ieee.org/conferences_events/conferences/publishing/templates.html
The report should contain the following sections, with clear evidence of novelty, significance, and impact throughout:
Abstract: 
Summarise the project problem, aims, datasets, methods, principal findings, and practical or research contribution.
Introduction: 
Motivate the topic, explain why the problem matters, state the project objectives, and present the research question(s). The novelty of the work should be clearly stated here.
Related Work: 
Critically evaluate relevant academic work, identify limitations in existing approaches, and explain how your project is positioned differently or adds value.
Methodology: 
Describe the datasets, preprocessing, databases, modelling choices, evaluation strategy, technologies, and overall workflow. Include diagrams showing the full pipeline.
Results and Evaluation: 
Present the findings using tables, figures, and discussion. Explain what was discovered, how well the approach worked, and what the results mean.
Conclusions and Future Work: 
Reflect critically on what others can learn from your work, discuss limitations, explain the significance of your findings, and suggest realistic future extensions.
Bibliography: 
Provide a complete list of cited academic works and online materials. References should be included as in-text citations using IEEE style.
5.2 Project Presentation
As a team, you should create a video presentation of no more than 10 minutes. It should explain what you did, how you did it, why you did it, what you discovered, and why the work matters.
Although individual members may present different parts, each member of the team is expected to be able to present all aspects of the work individually and without assistance if required.
5.3 Code Artefact
You should create a zip or gz archive containing all relevant assets, such as program code, data-processing scripts, data dictionaries where appropriate, and system configuration details sufficient to understand and review the implementation.
5.4 Project Journal
Each member of each project team should maintain an individual journal over the course of the project. The journal should include a brief description of each task carried out, the time spent, the challenges encountered, and how those challenges were addressed.
Note: Submission of the journal is mandatory. Zero marks will be awarded to any team member for the entire project if a journal is not submitted.

Assessment interpretation: 
Novelty contributes 40% in total, significance and impact contribute 30% in total, and the remaining 30% is distributed across datasets, models, and paper quality. This means a technically correct project with weak originality or limited importance is unlikely to achieve the highest marks.
8. High-Level Grading Guidance
Excellent / H1 range: 
The work is original, important, well-executed, and convincingly argued. The novelty is clear, the impact is believable, and the technical pipeline is robust and well documented.
Very Good / H2.1 range: 
The work shows good originality and a clear purpose, with solid technical execution. There may be some weaknesses in depth, evaluation, or communication, but the contribution remains strong.
Good / H2.2 range: 
The project is competent and complete, but novelty or impact is only moderate. Technical work is acceptable, though some choices may be under-justified or limited.
Pass range: 
The project demonstrates a basic workable pipeline and some findings, but originality, significance, execution, or writing are limited.
Fail range: 
The project lacks clarity, originality, technical substance, or sufficient evidence. The submission may be incomplete, poorly justified, or inadequately communicated.
9. Academic Integrity
Any written work created by others must be properly cited and should be paraphrased or summarised where possible; otherwise, it should be placed in quotation marks. Figures not created by you should include an acknowledgement detailing the name(s) of the creator(s). Code found on the internet should not be claimed as your own; instead, a comment should be included in the source code indicating where it was obtained.
Students are strongly advised to familiarise themselves with the Guide to Academic Integrity produced by the NCI Library:
https://libguides.ncirl.ie/academicintegrity
All submissions will be electronically screened for evidence of academic misconduct, including plagiarism, collusion, and misrepresentation. Any submission showing evidence of such misconduct may be referred to the college's academic misconduct committee for disciplinary action.
